Material Types

NairnFEA and NairnMPM support a variety of material types. Details on each material are given on the OSUPDocs wiki for MPM Materials, for FEA Materials, and for traction law materials; this section just lists the available materials and the properties needed to use them in calculations.

A material or traction law is defined in scripting commands using a block that allows you to specify a new material type and define all its properties. The command block format is:

Material (id),(name),(type)
  (Property commands - one per line)
Done

where (id) is the ID or Label for the material created by the command, (name) is the material name entered as a string, and (type) is the material type, which is enter by material name or number in the table below.

The Material command should be followed by lines that contain the material properties. Each property should be on a separate line and when the last property is specified, enter a line with the single Done Command. To set a property, use the property name (case sensitive) followed by a space and then its value. For example, to set modulus of an isotropic material, use

E 2300

A few properties differ from the above (name,value) pair; those exceptions are in the material definitions.

Linear Viscoelastic, Isotropic (Type=6)

This material is a small-strain, linear viscoelastic material with time-dependent shear modulus given by a sum or relaxation times and a time-independent pressure-volume response. The pressure law can use small strain methods (with bulk modulus) or large-deformation Mie-Grüneisen equation of state (MGEOS). The input properties are:

  • pressureLaw - set to 0 to use small strain method (with provided K) or 1 to use MGEOS (with all its properties)
  • K - the time-independent bulk modulus (in MPa) (when pressureLaw=0)
  • C0 - the bulk wave speed in MGEOS (in m/sec) (when pressureLaw=1)
  • S1 - a parameter in MGEOS (dimensionless) (when pressureLaw=1)
  • S2 - a parameter in MGEOS (dimensionless) (when pressureLaw=1)
  • S3 - a parameter in MGEOS (dimensionless) (when pressureLaw=1)
  • gamma0 - a parameter in MGEOS (dimensionless) (when pressureLaw=1)
  • G0 - the steady-state (or long time) shear modulus (in MPa)
  • Gk - the shear modulus for the next relaxation time (in MPa)
  • tauk - the next relaxation time (in sec)
  • alpha - the thermal expansion coefficient (in ppm/C) (ignored for MGEOS when pressureLaw=1)
  • (other) - see the properties common to all materials (MPM only).

The relaxation shear modulus is given by G(t) = G0 + G1exp(-t/tau1) + G2exp(-t/tau2) + ... + Gntausexp(-t/taun), where n is the number of relation times entered (and that number must exactly match the number of Gk shear moduli entered).

History Data for Linear Viscoelastic, Isotropic Materials

This material tracks history for internal variables needed for implementation of linear viscoelastic properties, but currently those history variables are available for archiving. You can archive total volume change (J) and residual free expansion volume (Jres) in history variables 1 and 2 (Jres, however, is only tracked when using MGEOS).

Mooney-Rivlin Hyperelastic, Isotropic (Type=8)

The material type is a large-deformation, isotropic, hyperelastic material. The material properties are

  • G1 or G2 - the Mooney-Rivlin shear energy depends on two shear moduli (in MPa). Their sum in the low-strain shear modulus. If G2=0 (which is its default), the material is a Neohookean material.
  • K - the K modulus (in MPa), which is equal to the low-strain bulk modulus.
  • E an nu - alternative enter E (in MPa) and nu. These are converted to K and G=G1+G2; you can pick G2 (if not zero) to get G1=G-G2
  • UJOption - selects the dilational energy term U(J) and the possible integer values mean:
        0: U(J) = (K/2)((1/2)(J2-1) - ln J) (the default)
        1: U(J) = (K/2)(J-1)2
        2: U(J) = (K/2)(ln J)2
  • alpha - the thermal expansion coefficient (in ppm/C)
  • IdealRubber - this property should by 0 or 1 where 1 makes the material an ideal rubber elastic material.
  • (other) - see the properties common to all materials (MPM only).

History Data for Mooney-Rivlin Materials

  1. The volumetric ratio or J = V/V0 (i.e., the determinant of the deformation gradient)

The plastic strain stores the left Cauchy Green tensor.

Neo-Hookean Material

The material type is an isotropic, hyperelastic material. The material properties are:

  • K, G, Lame, E, and nu - these are the low-strain elastic properties for the material. You must enter K and G, E and nu, or Lame and G (moduli in MPa).
  • UJOption for an integer to select the an energy term U(J) and the possible options are:
        0: U(J) = (Lame/2)((1/2)(J2-1) - ln J) (the default)
        1: U(J) = (Lame/2)(J-1)2
        2: U(J) = (Lame/2)(ln J)2
  • alpha for thermal expansion coefficient in ppm/degree C.
  • (other) - see the properties common to all materials (MPM only).

From the first five properties, you must enter K and G, Lame and G, OR E and nu.

History Data for Neo-Hookean Material

  1. The volumetric ratio or J = V/V0 (i.e., the determinant of the deformation gradient)

The plastic strain stores the left Cauchy Green tensor.

Clamped Neo-Hookean Material (Type=29)

The material type is a large-deformation, isotropic, hyperelastic material. It yields when the tensile and/or compression elongation reaches critical values of 1+θs and/or 1-θc. The underlying elastic component of the deformation is a neo-Hookean material. After yielding, the mechanical properties soften in tension or harden in compression according to an entered hardening coefficient. The material properties are

  • CritTens and CritComp - enter critical strains θs and θc for critical strains for tension and compression.
  • xihard - hardening parameter ξ. The neo-Hookean moduli are scaled by exp(ξ(1-JP)) where JP is the plastic volume ratio.
  • Elastic - enter 0 to use neo-Hookean potential energy defined in paper that proposed this material or enter 1 to use Neo-Hookean material potential energy. This potential energy applies to elastic component of the deformation.
  • (other) - enter properties for the underlying Neo-Hookean material (note that the UJOption can only be changed when Elastic is 1; UJOption is always 1 when Elastic is 0).

History Data for Clamped Neo-Hookean Material

  1. The volumetric ratio or J = V/V0 (i.e., the determinant of the deformation gradient)
  2. The plastic volumetric ratio or JP = J/JE where JE is determinant of the elastic deformation gradient)

The plastic strain stores the left Cauchy Green tensor. You have to archive both strain and plastic strain for the visualization to be able to plot elastic and plastic strains.

Bistable, Isotropic Elastic (Type=10)

This material has two small-strain, isotopic states. You need to enter properties of the initial and deformed states and pick rule for state transformations:

  • K0 and Kd - bulk modulus of initial state and transformed state (in MPa).
  • G0 and Gd - shear modulus of initial state and transformed state (in MPa).
  • alpha0 and alphad - thermal expansion coefficient of initial state and transformed state (in ppm/degree C).
  • beta0 and betad - concentration expansion coefficient of initial state and transformed state (in strain/(wt fraction)).
  • D0 and Dd - diffusion constant of initial state and transformed state (in mm2/sec).
  • kCond0 and kCondd - thermal conductivity of initial state and transformed state (in W/(m-K)).
  • transition - set state transition to be by a dilation rule (if 1), a distortion rule (if 2), or Von Mises stress rule (if 3) (the default is dilation).
  • critical - critical value for the transition. The value is volumetric or deviatoric strain (in % for dilation or distortion rule) or stress (in MPa for Von Mises stress rule).
  • DeltaVOffset - offset volumetric strain in the transformed state. This property only applies for dilation rule (see above); it is ignored for distortion rule.
  • reversible - set to "yes" (or 1) to make the transition reversible and "no" (or 0) to make it irreversible (the default is reversible).
  • (other) - see the properties common to all materials (MPM only).

History Data for Bistable, Isotropic Elastic Materials

  1. Will be 0 for the initial state and 1 for the deformed state after a transition.

Rigid Material (Type=11)

The material is actually two materials in one, depending on the value for its SetDirection property. If SetDirection is 0 through 7, the material is not really a material. Instead material points with such a material will assign grid-based boundary conditions that translate with the particle. If SetDirection is 8, the material will correspond to actual material points that interact with other material points only through multimaterial contact laws. The material properties are:

  • direction - values 0 through 7 (binary 0 through 0x111) create dynamic velocity boundary conditions in the x, y, and/or z axis according to three bits in the binary value (e.g.,1 or binary 0x001 to set velocity in the x direction). A setting of 8 creates rigid particles that can implement multimaterial contact contact.
  • temperature - set to 1 to create dynamic temperature boundary conditions (it requires direction 0 to 7).
  • concentration - set to 1 to create dynamic concentration boundary conditions (it requires direction 0 to 7).
  • SettingFunction, SettingFunction2, or SettingFunction3 - by providing user-defined functions of time, position, and time step, the rigid particle velocity setting can vary. The functions should evaluate to the velocity in mm/s. For SetDirection 1 to 7 the functions must be given in order and apply to the set velocities in order. For SetDirection of 8, the three functions are for setting x, y, and z directions and zero to three can be used.
  • SettingFunctionX, SettingFunctionY, or SettingFunctionY - synonyms for SettingFunction, SettingFunction2, or SettingFunction3, respectively.
  • ValueFunction - by entering user-defined functions of time, time step, and position, the rigid particle temperature and concentration can vary. The function should evaluate to the temperature or concentration potential.
  • mirrored should be set to -1 or +1 to indicate that rigid particles with SetDirection = 1 to 7 are setting a velocity on the minimum edge (-1) or maximum edge (+1) of the object along the moving direction. This property can improve the performance of velocity boundary conditions as explained on the OSUPdocs wiki. The default setting of 0 means to not use mirroring. Note that when ExtrapolateRigid option is activated, this property is ignored.

Hill Plastic, Orthotropic Material (Type=15)

This material is a small-strain, elastic-plastic material based on an orthotropic material but has plasticity determined by a built-in, anisotropic criterion for yielding:

[ F(σyy - σzz)2 + G(σxx - σzz)2 + H(σyy - σxx)2 + 2Lτyz2 + 2Mτxz2 + 2Nτxy2 ]1/2 - (1 + Kεpn)

where K and n are dimensionless hardening law properties and F through N are determined from yield stress in each material direction. The material properties are:

  • yldxx - yield stress from uniaxial tension test the material's x direction (in MPa)
  • yldyy - yield stress from uniaxial tension test the material's y direction (in MPa)
  • yldzz - yield stress from uniaxial tension test the material's z direction (in MPa)
  • yldxy - yield stress from pure shear test in the material's x-y plane (in MPa)
  • yldyz - yield stress from pure shear test in the material's y-z plane (in MPa)
  • yldxz - yield stress from pure shear test in the material's x-z plane (in MPa)
  • khard - hardening term (see above)
  • nhard - exponent on cumulative plastic strain in hardening term (see above)
  • (other) - see Linear Elastic, Orthotropic material for all other properties.

History Data for Hill Plastic, Orthotropic Material

  1. The cumulative equivalent plastic strain defined as the sum of sqrt(2/3)||dεp||.

The plastic strain will have the plastic strain. You have to archive both strain and plastic strain for the visualization to be able to plot elastic and plastic strains.

Mie-Grüneisen Equation of State Materials (Type=25 or Type=17)

This material is isotropic with plasticity and find pressure using the Mie-Grüneisen equation of state by large deformation methods. It is a large-deformation, hyperelastic-plastic methods for shear and yielding can use any hardening law for plasticity. Note: the Type=17 material used to be a small-strain material, but is now deleted and both types now use large deformation methods. The material properties are:

  • C0 - the bulk wave speed (in m/sec).
  • S1 - a parameter in Mie-Grüneisen equation of state (dimensionless).
  • S2 - a parameter in Mie-Grüneisen equation of state (dimensionless).
  • S3 - a parameter in Mie-Grüneisen equation of state (dimensionless).
  • gamma0 - a parameter in Mie-Grüneisen equation of state (dimensionless).
  • G - the shear modulus at zero pressure and the reference temperature (in MPa).
  • Hardening - this property selects the hardening law. It should be followed by entering all yielding properties required by the hardening law within the material's definition block.
  • (other) - see the properties common to all materials. This material requires heat capacity Cv and a StressFreeTemp in K.

In this material, the pressure is related to the volumetric compression strain (x = -ΔV/V0) by the Mie-Grüneisen equation of state. When x>0, the material is in compression and pressure is

P = ρ0C02x(1 - 0.5 γ0 x) / (1 - S1x - S2x2 - S3x3)2 + ρ0γ0e

where e is the internal energy. When x<0, the material is in tension and the pressure changes to

P = ρ0C02x + ρ0γ0e

The first term uses the low-strain bulk modulus, which is ρ0C02.

This equation of state automatically handles thermal expansion (assuming a valid Cv was provided) and therefore you do not need to enter a thermal expansion coefficient.

History Data for Mie-Grüneisen Equation of State Material

All hardening law will have one or more history variables. This material then tracks relative volume (J = V/V0) in the first history variable after hardening law history variables.

The plastic strain stores the elastic, left Cauchy Green tensor. You have to archive both strain and plastic strain for the visualization to be able to plot elastic and plastic strains.

Isotropic, Small-Strain Material with Anisotropic Damage (Type=50)

The material is an isotropic, small strain material and currently only in OSParticulas. Once damage initiates (using any available initiation law), the material develops a crack and propagates damage using anisotropic damage mechanics. The damage process is controlled by any available softening law. The properties are:

  • Initiation - this property selects the damage initiation law. It should be followed by entering all properties required by the damage initiation law within the material's definition block. The default is maximum principle stresses law.
  • SofteningI - this property selects the mode I softening law. It should be followed by entering all properties required by the softening law within the material's definition block. Each property should be prefaced with "I-". For example, to enter mode I law toughness for "Gc", enter it ias "I-Gc". The default is linear softening law.
  • SofteningII - this property is same as "SofteningI" except it selects the mode II softening law and properties for this law should be prefaced with "II-".
  • (other) - see Isotropic material for all properties of the underlying small-strain, isotropic material, except this softening material requires use of largeRotation property set to 1.

The particle plastic strain will archive the total cracking strain in the global axis system. The plastic energy will archive the total amount of energy released by damage to the particle.

History Data for Isotropic, Small-Strain Materials with Anisotropic Damage

  1. The maximum crack opening strain
  2. The maximum crack shear strain
  3. cos(α) in 2D or α in 3D for normal to the crack plane
  4. sin(α) in 2D or β in 3D for normal to the crack plane
  5. 0 in 2D or γ in 3D for normal to the crack plane
  6. 0, 1, or 2 to indicate undamaged, damage propagation, or post failure (decohesion) state of the particle.
  7. A scaling factor used internally to scale softening law response depending on particle volume, crack plane orientation, and initiation stress.

History variables 3-5 are storing Euler angles α, β, and γ (in radians) for a Z-Y-Z rotation scheme; only α is needed for 2D and it is stored as cos(α) and sin(α) instead of as angles.

Triangular Traction Law Material (Type=12)

The traction law has a triangular shape. There are separate traction laws for opening displacement (mode I) and sliding displacements (mode II):

  • sigmaI and sigmaII - the peak stress or cohesive stress of the traction law (σ) (in MPa).
  • JIc and JIIc - the toughness of the traction law or area under the traction law curve (Jc) (in J/m2)
  • delIc and delIIc - the maximum or critical opening displacement (δc) at which debonding occurs (in mm)
  • delpkI and delpkII - the relative displacement at the location of the peak in the triangular law (relative to δc and therefore dimensionless from 0 to 1).
  • kIe and kIIe - the initial elastic slope (k) (in MPa/mm).
  • nmix - the exponent used in the mixed-mode failure criterion Enter nmix<=0 to choose infinity, which is decoupled failure criterion (see below).

You must then enter exactly two of the first three properties for each mode (because they are interrelated). You must enter zero or one for the next two properties. If one is provided, the other will be calculated. If neither k nor δpeak are entered, the peak will be located at δpeak = 0.225926299δc.

Failure occurs when

(GI/JIc)nmix + (GII/JIIc)nmix = 1

where GI and GII are areas under mode I and mode II traction laws up to current displacements. If nmix<=0, failure occurs when either mode reaches its critical COD.

Linear Elastic Traction Law Material (Type=13)

This traction law creates a linear elastic traction law that never fails and therefore never releases energy. The only properties are the slopes for normal and shear opening.

  • kIe and kIIe - the slope of the linear elastic tractional law (k) (in MPa/mm).

If either slope is left out, it will be set to zero. To create a linear elastic traction law that fails at some critical cod, use a triangular traction law material instead.

Cubic Traction Law Material (Type=14)

The traction law uses a cubic function:

σ = (27/4) σmax (δ/δc)(1-(δ/δc))2.

that was designed to have zero slope at the critical COD. There are separate traction laws for opening displacement (mode I) and sliding displacements (mode II):

  • sigmaI and sigmaII - the peak stress or cohesive stress of the traction law (σ) (in MPa).
  • JIc and JIIc - the toughness of the traction law or area under the traction law curve (Jc) (in J/m2).
  • delIc and delIIc - the maximum or critical opening displacement (δc) at which debonding occurs (in mm).
  • nmix - the exponent used in the mixed-mode failure criterion. Enter nmix<=0 to choose infinity, which is decoupled failure criterion.

You must then enter exactly two of the first three properties for each mode. The failure criterion under possible mixed-mode conditions is identical to the one used in the triangular traction law.

Trilinear Traction Law Material (Type=20)

This traction law is a piecewise-linear law with two break points.There are separate traction laws for opening displacement (mode I) and sliding displacements (mode II):

  • sigmaI, sigmaII, JIc, JIIc, delIc, delIIc, kIe, kIIe, delpkI, delpkII, and nmix - these properties are defined in the Triangular Traction Law and they all mean the same thing for this traction law, except that they now apply to the first break point in the trilinear law at (δ1,σ1).
  • sigmaI2 and sigmaII2 - the stress at the second break point in the traction traction law (σ2) (in MPa).
  • delpkI2 and delpkII2 - the relative displacement at the location of the second peak in the trilinear law (relative to δc and therefore dimensionless from 0 to 1).

Excluding nmix, you must enter exactly 5 of the seven properties for each mode. Furthermore, if one of the 5 is kIe (or kIIe for mode II), then either sigmaI or delpkI (but not both) must be specified as well.

The failure under mixed-mode loading uses the same criterion explained above.

CoupledSawTooth Traction Law Material (Type=23)

This traction law also has a triangular shape except that cod and traction are now effective terms that are related to normal and tangential components of traction and displacement:

Traction = Teff where Teff2 = Tn2 + Tt2
cod = δeff where δeff2 = δn2 + δt2

The properties are:

  • sigmaI, JIc, delIc, kIe, and delpkI - these properties are defined in the Triangular Traction Law and they all mean the same thing for this traction law, except they apply now to effective traction and critical effective cod rather than mode I traction and critical cod. Mode II parameters and nmix are ignored.

Pressure Traction Law Material (Type=26)

This traction law creates a constant stress that is normal to the crack surface. It models a pressure loaded crack. The stress can be entered in one of two ways:

  • stress - enter constant normal stress; use negative stress for a pressure loaded crack (in MPa).
  • function - Alternatively, the stress can be entered as a user-defined function of time (in MPa).
  • minCOD - specify a minimum, normal, crack opening displacement below which no pressure is applied (in mm). It is ignored if it is negative (default is -1)

Contact Law that Ignores Contact (Type=60)

This law is not actually a contact law. This law ignores contact on explicit crack surfaces. For multimaterial contact, this law will revert to a single velocity field as if they was not a material interface. The law has no properties that can be set.

This law will give poor results for cracks that are in contact, unless those cracks never experience contact. For multimaterial mode simulations, this mode reverts to a single velocity field. For simulations with more than two materials where some contact by other contact laws and others should use single velocity fields, the better approach than using this law is to use the shareMatField (matID) in materials that should share the same field.

Coulomb Friction Contact Law (Type=61)

This law implements simple Coulomb friction law where sliding traction is proportional to the contacting nomral traction:

Sslide = N

and is the dynamic coefficient of friction. If the friction coefficient is entered as a negative number, then surfaces stick when in contact but move freely when separated. The properties for this law are:

  • coeff for the dynamic coefficient of friction (default is 0).
  • coeffStatic for the static coefficient of friction (default is -1).

If the optional static coefficient of friction is changed to a positive number, it must be greater than the dynamic coefficient. The sliding will begin when it overcomes the static frictional force, but thereafter will slide with the dynamic coefficient of friction.

|Material Tables|

Coulomb Friction with Adhesion Contact Law

This law implements Coulomb friction law with adhesion. If the surface are in contact, the frictional sliding force is

Sslide = Sa + N

where Sa is the tangential adhesion strength and is the dynamic coefficient of friction. If the surfaces are not in contact, the surface continue to stick as long as:

(Sstick/Sa)2 + (N/Na)2 < 1

where Na is the normal adhesion strength. If this criterion is not met, the suraces move freely with zero tractions. The properties for this law are:

  • coeff for the dynamic coefficient of friction (default is 0).
  • coeffStatic for the static coefficient of friction (default is -1).
  • Sa for tangential adhesion strength (in MPa) (default is 0).
  • Na for normal adhesion strength (in MPa) (default is 0).

If the optional static coefficient of friction is changed to a positive number, it must be greater than the dynamic coefficient. The sliding will begin when it sliding force overcomes the frictional force calculated from static coefficient, but thereafter will slide with force found using the dynamic coefficient of friction.

Linear Imperfect Interface Contact Law (Type=62)

This law implements an imperfect interface with tractions that depend linearly on crack opening displacements, although slopes can be different in tension or compression:

TN = DntδN     when separated
TN = DncδN     when in contact
TS = DtδS

The properties are the three interface parameters:

  • Dnt - the interfacial stiffness in tension (in MPa/mm) (default -1)
  • Dnc - the interfacial stiffness in compression (in MPa/mm). This property is optional and lets you specify a different stiffness for compression (interfaces in contact) then for tension (interfaces separated). If Dnc is omitted, the stiffnesses will be the same in tension and compression (i.e., Dnc equal to Dnt).
  • Dt - the interfacial stiffness in shear (in MPa mm) (default -1)

Although an interface becomes perfect as the interface parameters approach infinity, high interfacial stiffness make the problem numerically unstable. To nmodel a perfect interface in any direction, set the interface parameter to -1, instead of using a high value.

Linear Hardening (number=1)

In linear hardening the yield stress is given by

σY = σY0 + Epα = σY0(1 + Kα)

where σY0 is the initial yield stress, Ep is the plastic modulus, K is hardening coefficient, and α is cumulative, equivalent plastic strain. The input properties are:

  • yield the initial yield stress (in MPa).
  • Ep - the plastic modulus (in MPa). The default is 0.0 which results in an elastic-perfectly plastic material.
  • Khard - alternatively, you can enter this dimensionless parameter for hardening. It is only used if Ep is not entered.

History Data for Linear Hardening

  1. The cumulative equivalent plastic strain, α, defined as the sum of sqrt(2/3)||dεp||.

Nonlinear Hardening (number=2)

σY = σY0(1 + Kα)n

where K and n are dimensionless hardening parameters. The input properties are:

  • yield - the initial yield stress (in MPa).
  • Khard - coefficient for nonlinear hardening (dimensionless).
  • nhard - exponent parameter in the nonlinear hardening (dimensionless).

In this alternate nonlinear hardening the yield stress is given by

σY = σY0(1 + Kαn)

Johnson-Cook Hardening (number=3)

The Johnson-Cook hardening law is:

σy = (Ajc + Bjc εpnjc) [1 + Cjc ln(dεp/ep0jc) ] (1 - Trmjc)

where εp is equivalent plastic strain, dεp is plastic strain rate, and the reduced temperature (Tr) is given by

Tr = (T - T0) / (Tmjc - T0)

and T0 is the current stress free temperature. The law properties are:

  • Ajc - the initial yield stress at reference rate and temperature (in MPa).
  • Bjc - hardening term in the yield stress (in MPa).
  • njc - exponent for power-law hardening term (dimensionless).
  • Cjc - coefficient for rate-dependent term (dimensionless).
  • ep0jc - reference strain rate for reference yield stress in Ajc (in sec-1).
  • Tmjc - the material's melting point (in degrees K).
  • mjc - exponent in the thermal term (dimensionless).

History Data for Johnson-Cook Material

  1. The cumulative equivalent plastic strain, α, defined as the sum of sqrt(2/3)||dεp||.

Steinberg-Cochran-Lund Hardening (number=4)

In this hardening law, both the shear modulus and the yield stress change. The shear modulus is given by

G = G0 [ 1 + (Gp'/G0)(P/η1/3) + (GT'/G0)(T-T0) ]

where η = 1/(1-x) and x = -ΔV/V0. Here T0 is the reference temperature for the material that is set using the StressFreeTemp command. This yield stress is given by:

σy = σ0 (1 + β εpn) G

where εp is the equivalent plastic strain and G is current shear modulus from above. The yield stress is limited to a maximum yield stress. The properties for this law are:

  • yield - the initial yield stress (σ0) at zero pressure and the reference temperature (in MPa).
  • yieldMax - maximum yield stress (in MPa).
  • betahard - yield stress hardening term (dimensionless).
  • nhard - exponent on cumulative plastic strain in hardening term (dimensionless).
  • GPpG0 - Gp'/G0 term for pressure dependence of shear modulus (in units MPa-1 or 0 to omit pressure dependence in shear modulus).
  • GTpG0 - GT'/G0 term for temperature dependence of shear modulus (in units K-1 or 0 to omit temperature dependence in shear modulus).

History Data for SCGL Hardening Law

  1. The cumulative equivalent plastic strain, α, defined as the sum of sqrt(2/3)||dεp||.

Steinberg-Lund Hardening Law (number=5)

This hardening law has a rate- and temperature-dependent yield stress given by:

σy = {YT(dεp/dt,T) + σ0(1 + β εpn)} G

This law is identical to the SCGL law except for the new rate- and temperature-dependent term YT(dεp/dt,T), which is only defined in inverse form:

dεp(YT,T)/dt = { (C2/YT) + (1/C1)exp[(2Uk/(kT))(1-(YT/YP))2] }-1

Material Properties Common For All Material Types

There are some material properties that are common to all material types, although not all materials will use them all. The categories of common properties are:

  • Basic Properties
  • Fracture Toughness Properties
  • Crack Propagation Properties
  • Contact Properties
  • Artificial Viscosity

Basic Properties

These are basic material properties

  • rho - the density (in g/cm3)
  • Cv - the constant strain heat capacity (in J/(kg-K)). You do not need to enter a constant pressure heat capacity (Cp) because it is not needed. Some materials will output Cp by using thermodynamic relations for the difference between Cp and Cv, but Cp is never needed in calculations.
  • kCond - the thermal conductivity for isotropic materials (in W/(m-K))
  • csat - the saturation concentration entered as a weight fraction
  • beta - the concentration expansion coefficient for isotropic materials (in strain/(wt fraction))
  • D - the diffusion constant for isotropic materials (in mm2/sec)
  • largeRotation - to use (1) or not use (0, the default) polar decomposition when evaluating rotations. This option is only allowed for linear elastic and elastic-plastic small strain materials. Option 1 uses polar decomposition to evaluate incremental rotation and uses that rotation to update strains and stresses. Option 0 use approximations of polar decomposition to rotate strain and stress updates (standard hypoelasticity). Both methods work for large rotations (provided the rotations are incrementally small). Option 1 may be more accurate while options 0 is more efficient.
  • matDamping - sets custom particle damping to apply only to particles of this material. Its value replaces global particle damping setting and must be a constant (function of time not allowed).
  • matPIC - sets custom PIC damping to apply only to particles of this material. Its value replaces the global PIC damping setting.
  • color (red),(green),(blue),(alpha) - sets the color of the material with the four arguments being RGB and alpha values between 0.0 and 1.0. A single argument can be used instead to set gray level between 0.0 and 1.0 (with alpha=1.0). Three arguments set red, green, and blue with alpha=1.0. If no color is provided, a color will be picked from the current spectrum using the material number.

Fracture Toughness Properties

These properties set material properties that determine the fracture toughness of the material and control various aspects of crack propagation. Note that whether or not the properties are used depends on the selected criterion for crack growth.

  • JIc or JIc - critical mode I or mode II energy release rate (in J/m2).
  • KIc or KIIc - critical mode I or mode II stress intensity factor (in MPa m0.5).
  • KIexp or KIIexp - the exponent p or q in the elliptical criteria for crack growth.
  • delIc or delIIc - critical crack opening displacements in mode I and mode II.
  • initTime - time to start crack growth (in ms).
  • speed - define the constant crack speed (in m/sec).
  • maxLength - define the maximum length of the crack (in mm). The crack growth will stop when this length is reached.
  • nmix - a parameter used in some mixed-mode failure laws.
  • gamma, p, or gain - still in development and not meant for general use.

Crack Propagation Properties

The criterion, direction, and traction properties (and the analogous alternate propagation properties) are set with following XML commands (instead of standard property commands):

  • criterion - set crack growth criterion for this material (see options in Propagate Command).
  • direction - set optional crack growth direction for this material (see options in Propagate Command).
  • traction - set optional traction law to create for crack propagation in this material by ID or number (see Propagate Command for details).
  • altcriterion is identical to criterion, but applies instead to the alternate crack growth criterion in the AltPropagate Command.
  • altdirection is identical to direction, but applies instead to the alternate crack growth direction in the AltPropagate Command.
  • alttraction is identical to traction, but applies instead to the alternate crack growth traction law material in the AltPropagate Command.
  • xGrow and yGrow - specify a unit vector for a constant crack growth direction. They only apply for crack propagation with criterion 2 and then only when that criterion selects the default propagation direction.
  • constantTip - set to 0 or 1 where 1 means the crack tip will always uses its initial material even if the crack is in or moves to another material

Contact Properties

These commands allow custom contact properties for each material pair in multimaterial mode MPM.

  • Contact - a Contact material property can define custom contact law for multimaterial mode MPM contact between the current material and another material.
  • shareMatField (matID) - set to another material (by material ID) to share material velocity field with that material. Two (or more) materials sharing the same velocity field will move with perfect contact (i.e., in a single velocity field) among themselves, but can interact with other materials in different velocity fields by contact or interface laws. Materials sharing velocity fields must be compatible (e.g., all rigid or all nonrigid) and the material in (matID) must not be sharing it's field.

The following are deprecated material properties to set contact properties:

  • Friction (deprecated) - a Friction command within a material definition can define custom frictional properties for multimaterial mode MPM contact between the current material and another material.
  • Interface (deprecated) - an Interface command within a material definition can define custom imperfect interface parameters properties for multimaterial mode MPM contact between the current material and another material.

Artificial Viscosity

Artificial viscosity adds a pressure, Q, related to velocity gradient on the particle, but only when it is compressing. The equation is:

Q = Δx*|Dkk|*(A1*C + A2*Δx*|Dkk|)

where Δx is the cell size of the mesh, |Dkk| is the relative volume change rate, C is the bulk wave speed in the material, and A1 and A2 are adjustable constants.

  • ArtificialVisc - set to "on" or "off" to determine if artificial viscosity is on.
  • avA1 - the A1 parameter in artificial viscosity (dimensionless, default 0.2)
  • avA2 - the A2 parameter in artificial viscosity (dimensionless, default 2.0)

The artificial viscosity option is only supported in some materials (as documented here). If you use these commands in a material that does not support, an error will result.
